Viewing Historical Imagery

This tutorial explains the basics of viewing historical imagery within Google Earth. You will learn how to view historical imagery for the National Stadium in Beijing: The largest steel structure in the world, it was built for the 2008 Summer Olympics.

By default, Google Earth displays the highest quality imagery available. You can view historical imagery so that you can see how places have changed over time. You can view imagery from different dates, click back and forward between the different imagery, and play an animation of all the available imagery for a location.

The image below shows the highest quality imagery for the Beijing National Stadium, which is what you automatically see in Google Earth:

In this exercise, you will view historical imagery for the National Stadium in Beijing. To do this:

  1. Go to the Beijing National Stadium. For the purpose of this exercise, the quickest way to do this is to enter the following latitude and longitude coordinates in the Fly To field in the Search panel:

    39 59'28.83"N 116 23'26.30"E

    Press Enter. Google Earth flies to the National Stadium in Beijing. (If you prefer, you can search for this location instead of using the coordinates listed above. Learn more in the user guide or this tutorial).
  2. Click the Clock button in the toolbar. A timeline appears in the top left of the 3D viewer, marked by small gray lines to indicate the different imagery available for this location. You can see that the slider is automatically positioned at the far right of the timeline, showing that you are viewing the best imagery available.

Note - You can also access the timeline by clicking View > Historical Imagery.


  2. To go back in time, click and drag the slider to the left along the timeline. Or, to move quickly between the different satellite images, click the Forward or Back buttons above the slider.
  3. Release your mouse from the slider when the date above the timeline indicates the following dates:
    • February 8, 2007 - You can see the stadium under construction, with only its steel framework visible (see image below).


    • May 6, 2005 - You can see the stadium in its initial phase of construction.


    • Oct 23, 2003 - You can see the site of the stadium, several months before construction began in December 2003.


  4. You can use the Zoom in and Zoom out buttons to get a closer look at the timeline, and see more easily the imagery that's available within a shorter time range.

For more examples of historical imagery, here are some ideas for where to go next:

  • Las Vegas: Fly to "The Strip, Las Vegas, NV" and check out how Las Vegas has changed over time.
  • San Francisco: View historical imagery from as far back as 1946.
  • Berlin Hauptbahnhof: Watch the construction of Europe's largest two-level railway station, which opened in May 2006.
